The Four Color Theorem: A Historical Journey

1852

Francis Guthrie's Discovery

While coloring a map of English counties, Guthrie noticed only four colors were needed.

1879

Kempe's "Proof"

Alfred Kempe published what was believed to be a valid proof using chain arguments.

Kempe Chains: Paths of alternating colors that can be swapped without conflicts.
1890

Heawood Finds the Flaw

After 11 years, Percy Heawood discovered Kempe chains could interfere with each other.

The Flaw: When a vertex has degree 5, Kempe chains aren't independent.
1976

Computer-Assisted Proof

Appel & Haken proved the theorem by checking 1,936 configurations with a computer.

The Four Color Theorem: Theory & Algorithms

Formal Statement

The Four Color Theorem: Every planar graph is 4-colorable. That is, the vertices of any planar graph can be colored using at most four colors such that no two adjacent vertices share the same color.

Algorithms

DSATUR

Selects vertices by saturation degree - the number of different colors in their neighborhood.

Welsh-Powell

Orders vertices by degree and colors greedily in that order.

Backtracking

Exhaustive search with pruning to find optimal coloring.

Kempe Chains

Swaps colors along alternating paths to reduce chromatic number.
